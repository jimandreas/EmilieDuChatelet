Yes — the 1759 edition contains substantial original work by du Châtelet
The copies on Gallica absolutely contain her amplifications. The published work contains, after the publisher's note and Voltaire's preface, du Châtelet's translation of the Principia, followed by her own personal contribution under the heading Synthèse commentée et analyse des Principia par la marquise du Châtelet, comprising two parts: the Exposition abrégée du Système du Monde and the Solution analytique des principaux problèmes qui concernent le Système du Monde. OpenEdition
The first volume covers the first two books of the Principia on the movement of bodies in general, and the second volume covers Book III on the "System of the World," with the two commentaries appended. Cairn.info
What she actually added
Her commentary was enormous — it comprised almost two-thirds of Volume II of her edition. Wikipedia This wasn't just footnotes. She did several distinct things:
1. A plain-language exposition of the system of the world. Her Exposition abrégée provided three ways to understand Newton's physics. In the first, she gave the reader a clear understanding of the basic ideas and the logic behind Newton's physics, avoiding the technical language of science and mathematics. EBSCO This historical contextual section didn't exist in Newton at all — Newton only referenced Kepler and didn't discuss prior theories. OpenEdition Scholars have noted it was one of the earliest recognized works of scientific popularization.
2. Analytical solutions to disputed problems. She wrote analytical solutions to key disputed aspects of Newton's theory of universal gravitation, and included summaries of Clairaut's work on the shape of the Earth and Daniel Bernoulli's work on tidal effects. Royal Society Open Science
3. Incorporation of the latest science. She included references to scientific works published as recently as two years before her death Education — updating Newton with current results, like Bernoulli's work on tides. Newton had suggested gravitational attraction would flatten the Earth's poles, and du Châtelet used Clairaut's memoir confirming this hypothesis and providing more accurate approximations. Wikipedia
4. A Leibniz–Newton synthesis. Du Châtelet approached the Principia not as a monument to Newton, but as a text to be worked on — she placed Newton's discoveries in the context of other scientists, including before and after him, championing a collaborative model of science. ResearchGate Notably, she included her derivation of the notion of conservation of energy from Newton's principles of mechanics. Wikipedia
5. Mathematical modernization. She didn't do a word-for-word translation — she developed entire sentences and gave them meaning Cairn.info, and critically, she converted Newton's geometric proofs into the more modern analytical (algebraic/calculus) notation that was becoming standard on the continent. This analytical commentary was conceived by du Châtelet as an integral part of her translation from the very beginning of the project. Cairn.info

I should stress upfront that I'm not a lawyer, so for anything with real stakes you'd want to confirm this with a legal professional — but the landscape here is fairly clear.
The underlying text: no copyright issues
Du Châtelet died in 1749 and her work was published in 1759. The text itself — her translation, her commentary, her handwritten words — has been in the public domain for centuries. There is zero copyright on the content. You can translate it, republish it, annotate it, blog about it however you like.
The digital scans: this is where it gets nuanced
The tricky part is the images — the BnF's digital photographs of the manuscript pages. There are two legal frameworks to think about:
In the EU/France, the BnF invokes the EU's sui generis database right on its Gallica collection, which protects the investment in digitizing and organizing materials. The BnF's policy is that non-commercial use is free of charge, provided the source is acknowledged as "BnF" or "Bibliothèque nationale de France." BnF Commercial use — meaning use in a publication for sale, exhibition, audiovisual production, paid service, or promotional medium — requires authorization and payment of a fee. BnF
So the key question for you is whether your blog counts as commercial or non-commercial:

A personal blog with no monetization, no ads, no paywall — that should fall squarely under the free non-commercial use, as long as you credit the BnF/Gallica as the source.
A blog with ads, affiliate links, Patreon, or any revenue stream — this starts looking "commercial" under the BnF's definition and you'd technically need to contact their reproduction department.
The BnF states that reproduction and communication of materials is free for non-profit use, and for commercial use, permission must be requested. Gallica

In the US, the legal picture is more favorable. No database right exists in the United States — originality is the sine qua non of copyright, and a faithful photographic reproduction of a public domain work doesn't create a new copyright. Wikipedia The landmark case Bridgeman Art Library v. Corel Corp. established exactly this: a slavishly faithful reproduction of a public domain work doesn't generate copyright. Some commentators have noted that similar reasoning likely applies in the UK and EU for photographic reproductions, though the database right adds a separate layer. IMSLP Forums
Practical guidance for a blog project
If you're based in Germany and publishing a blog:

Credit Gallica/BnF on every manuscript image you use. Something like "Source: Bibliothèque nationale de France, Gallica — Fr. 12266" beneath each image. This is the minimum requirement for non-commercial use.
Your own translations are entirely yours. A fresh English translation of du Châtelet's 18th-century French is an original creative work — you hold copyright on your translation.
If your blog is non-commercial, you're almost certainly fine under the BnF's own stated terms. The spirit of Gallica is to make these materials accessible.
If your blog generates any revenue, the safest path would be to email reproduction@bnf.fr and explain your project. Given that this is scholarly/educational work on an 18th-century woman scientist, they may well be cooperative — the BnF has a stated interest in promoting this kind of engagement.
Consider also the Linda Hall Library scans as an alternative or supplementary source — US-based institutions often have more permissive terms for digital reproductions of public domain works.
The 2015 Toulmonde critical edition (the modern scholarly edition of the manuscript) would be under copyright as a recent editorial work. Don't reproduce material from that without permission — but working directly from Gallica's scans of the original 1759 print or the 1749 manuscripts is a different matter entirely.

The short version: for a non-commercial blog with proper attribution to the BnF, you're on very solid ground. The content is public domain, your translations would be your own work, and the BnF explicitly permits non-commercial reuse of their digitized materials.
