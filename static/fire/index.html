On the Nature and Propagation of Fire

Two Essays — Émilie du Châtelet & Voltaire

Ignea convexi vis, & sine pondere cœli / Emicuit, summâque locum sibi legit in arce. Ovid, Metamorphoses I.26–27 — epigraph to du Châtelet's Dissertation (1744)

The Competition

In the autumn of 1737, the Académie royale des sciences of Paris announced the subject of its annual prize: Quelle est la nature du feu, et quelles sont ses propriétés ? — What is the nature of fire, and what are its properties? The prize carried prestige second only to election to the Académie itself.

At Château de Cirey in Lorraine, Voltaire and his companion Émilie du Châtelet had established a serious scientific laboratory. Both were drawn to the competition — but, extraordinarily, each worked on their essay without informing the other. Du Châtelet composed her Dissertation at night, in secret, submitting it independently under her own name.

When the results were announced in 1738, neither Voltaire nor du Châtelet won the prize. The first prize was divided among three recipients including Leonhard Euler, whose theory that fire was an elastic fluid within bodies satisfied the judges. However, the Académie's commissioners expressly noted that both essays merited publication alongside the winning entries — a rare honour. Du Châtelet thereby became the first woman to have a scientific paper published by the Académie royale des sciences.

Both essays were eventually published in 1744 by Prault fils (Paris) in a volume that also included correspondence and related texts. Du Châtelet revised and expanded her essay for the 1744 edition.

The Two Essays

The essays represent sharply divergent approaches. Voltaire worked largely within the Newtonian framework, treating fire as an elementary substance with measurable properties. Du Châtelet, more theoretically ambitious, challenged prevailing assumptions about fire's relationship to heat and light, argued that the universal effect of fire is the expansion of volume in bodies, and engaged critically with Descartes, Newton, and Boerhaave.

The editorial notice to the Garnier edition (1879) of Voltaire's essay notes that his was "the only one containing truly new experiments," while acknowledging that du Châtelet's approach — proposing that light and heat derive from a single element whose particles move in different patterns — anticipated later experimental confirmation.

Émilie du Châtelet (1706–1749)

Dissertation sur la nature et la propagation du feu

Written in 1737–38 without Voltaire's knowledge. Revised and published 1744, Paris: Prault fils. 139 pages. The essay challenges received views on fire's relationship to heat and light, proposes volume expansion as fire's universal effect, and engages with the question of fire's weight and impenetrability.

Voltaire (François-Marie Arouet, 1694–1778)

Essai sur la nature du feu et sur sa propagation

Composed in summer 1737 at Cirey. First published in the prize volume of 1739. Reprinted in Voltaire's Œuvres complètes (Garnier, 1879, tome 22, pp. 282–325). Treats fire as an elementary substance and presents original experiments on heat, elasticity, and combustion.

On the Translations

The English translations presented in this edition are new scholarly renderings prepared for this project. They aim to be faithful to the scientific argument and the formal register of the originals while remaining accessible to modern readers. Eighteenth-century orthographic conventions in the French text (the long s ſ rendered as s, capitalisation of nouns, ligatures) are preserved from the printed editions.

Each section of the parallel editions links directly to the corresponding page in the digitised French original, allowing readers to verify the translation or study the source text in facsimile. Page numbers in gold superscript within the text refer to the pages of the 1744 Prault edition (du Châtelet) or the 1879 Garnier edition (Voltaire) as preserved in the respective digital libraries.
